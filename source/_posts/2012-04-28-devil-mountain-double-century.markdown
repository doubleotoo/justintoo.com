"This ride was <a href="http://www.quackcyclists.com/DMDResults.htm" target="_blank">first ridden</a>  by a handful of riders in July of 1996.

On this ride, you'll have the opportunity of climbing <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Diablo" target="_blank">Mt. Diablo</a> and <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Hamilton_(California)" target="_blank">Mt. Hamilton</a> in the San Ramon area.  Certainly 'not for the faint of heart', this ride is a major challenge for sure!!

Mt. Diablo was listed by BICYCLING magazine as one of the ten toughest climbs in America.  Mt. Diablo rises <strong>3,200 feet in 10.5 miles</strong> to a height of 3,849 feet.  Mt. Hamilton will have you climb <strong>2,100 feet in just 7 miles</strong> to the 4,100 foot peak.

Sierra Road will be your biggest test: not only is it incredibly steep, climbing <strong>1,800 Feet in 3.2 miles</strong>, but it comes after 150 miles!!

Most of this ride is very rural, very scenic, and very low on traffic.  The terrain is unspoiled and rugged. If you like to climb and are ready for a real challenge, this ride's for you!!" <a href="/blog/2012/04/22/devil-mountain-double-century-staff-ride#caltriplecrown-description">[1]</a>

<p>Another <em>normal</em> saturday.</p>

<p>Hmm...</p>

<p>Maybe not exactly <em>normal</em>. Just not hard to believe.</p>

<p>Waking up at 3 o'clock in the morning isn't exactly my choice cup of tea,
yet I still drink that poison on a regular basis. I think this is what you
call an <em>addiction</em>.</p>

<p><em>Hi, my name is Justin Too, and I am... uhm... an athlete?</em></p>

<hr class="soften">

#### Riding the Devil

<p>Last Saturday, I decided to participate in the <a href="http://www.quackcyclists.com/dmd.htm" target="_blank">Devil Mountain Double Century</a> staff ride, proudly hosted by the <a href="http://www.quackcyclists.com/" target="_blank">Quackcyclists</a>.</p>

<p>Considering my unfortunate bailout two weekends ago at the <u>Mulholland Double Century</u>, I already knew that I wouldn't be able to <em>Whip the Devil</em> this time around. Regardless, I didn't want to miss this great training opportunity. My goal: to the top of Mt. Hamilton (about 150 miles into the ride).</p>

<p></p>
<p>The landscape is lush with Spring flowers and the road has plenty of tree cover to make even a hot day pleasant.</p>

<p>But climbing up the narrow, bi-directional one lane road could be annoying, both for bicyclists and motorists, but thankfully, since it was still early in the morning, traffic wasn't an issue.</p>

<img src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/-nWqPr7QLbV8/T53LT0dMUSI/AAAAAAAAAWI/m1hetqXzvKw/s144/chocolatechipmuffins.jpg" width="288" height="261" class="image-right framed">

<p>Half-way up the climb, we passed a rest stop. This stop, however, wasn't for me. It was for a trail running event that was going on that morning. And boy, what a pleasure it is to see other <em>crazy</em> people already <em>out-and-about</em> and <em>active</em> on <em>an early morning adventure</em>.</p>

<p><u>Rest Stop #2</u> was at the top of the <a href="http://www.google.com/search?client=safari&rls=en&q=morgan+territory&ie=UTF-8&oe=UTF-8#hl=en&client=safari&rls=en&sclient=psy-ab&q=morgan+territory%2C+ca&oq=morgan+territory%2C+ca&aq=f&aqi=g3g-v1&aql=&gs_nf=1&gs_l=serp.3..0l3j0i15.4758.5004.0.5413.4.3.0.0.0.0.222.532.0j2j1.3.0.FaFBuauiyrs&pbx=1&bav=on.2,or.r_gc.r_pw.r_cp.r_qf.,cf.osb&fp=6f1aa300c3c2575e&biw=1318&bih=665" target="_blank">Morgan territory</a> climb at <strong>mile 51.8</strong>. I refilled one of my water bottles and downed a yummy chocolate chip muffin, to keep my stomach occupied during "The Plunge" down into the outskirts of Livermore (familiar territory).</p>

<hr class="soften" />

#### Altamont Pass and Patterson Pass

<p>The journey through <a href="http://www.google.com/search?client=safari&rls=en&q=altamont+pass,+ca&ie=UTF-8&oe=UTF-8" target="_blank">Altamont Pass</a> and <a href="http://www.google.com/search?client=safari&rls=en&q=patterson+pass,+ca&ie=UTF-8&oe=UTF-8" target="_blank">Patterson Pass</a> was exhausting. But the scenery more than made up for it! (Probably not, but let's just say so for my sanity's sake.)</p>

<p></p>
<p>And although I work close by to this area, I've never really taken the time to explore the <em>open country</em>. I felt like a happy little boy, riding through the hills, marveling at the ginormous wind mills.</p>

<p>The <em>mills</em> weren't spinning, though. <em>But thank goodness!</em> (Because spinning wind mill <em>equals</em> wind. And wind usually <em>equals</em> head-wind. And I <em>shudder</em> at that thought.)</p>

<p>After descending back into Livermore, I continued on <em>Tesla Road</em>, made the left turn onto <em>Mines Road</em>, took a quick bathroom break and ate some snacks at <u>Rest Stop #3</u>.</p>

<p>Then, leaving the rest stop, I made myself comfortable in the saddle, ready for the next 30 mile, <em>in-the-middle-of-no-where</em>, stretch to "The Junction".</p>

<p></p>
<p>
<em>The Junction</em> ended up being my final destination for the staff ride. My legs were feeling good, but the heat killed me. With a pulsating head and a parched mouth, I threw in the towel. <em>Hamilton, we'll meet another day</em>.</p>

<p>The two hours on Mines Road was met with <a href="http://www.wunderground.com/history/airport/KSJC/2012/4/21/DailyHistory.html?req_city=NA&req_state=NA&req_statename=NA" target="_blank">blistering heat</a>, one-degree off of the record high of 91 degrees Fahrenheit, set in 2009.</p>

<p>Another important lesson.</p>

<p>In Muholland, I discovered that there's another class of <em>climbing</em> out there.</p>

<blockquote>
Alice: How do you pronounce Alpe d'Huez?
<br/>
<br/>
Joe: Big *beep* hill.
</blockquote>

And during the DMD staff ride, I was reminded that <em>weather conditions</em> are an important factor for any outdoor adventure.
</p>

<p>I think God purposely gave me these two experiences to prepare me.</p>

<p><em>But I wonder what for...</em></p>

<p>At least for the immediate future, these lessons have already helped me in my training. On my checklist: heat, hills, and wind.</p>

<p>Am I missing anything?</p>

<p><em>Sleep deprivation.</em></p>

<p>Staff rides are usually hosted a week prior to the riding of the actual public event. This gives event volunteers an opportunity to ride the course, either for fun, for training, or, why not, for both!</p>

<p>The <a href="http://www.quackcyclists.com/DmdRegistered.cfm" target="_blank">public ride</a> was yesterday, Saturday, April 28.</p>

<p>My parents and I helped man the lunch rest stop at <a href="http://www.thejunctionbarandgrill.com/" target="_blank">"The Junction"</a>. (It's called a <em>Bar and Grill</em>. But it's not what you think. Definitely not <a href="http://www.chilis.com/" target="_blank">Chilis</a>.)</p>

<table style="width:auto;"><tr><td><a href="https://plus.google.com/photos/113607174221973452906/albums/5734369780570416145"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/-fT7GFxRGWWs/T54JrDIz1GI/AAAAAAAAAXI/KYRlTcsP17s/s640/DSC02847.JPG" height="360" width="640" /></a></td></tr><tr><td style="font-family:arial,sans-serif; font-size:11px; text-align:right"></td></tr></table>

<p></p>

<p>At <em>The Junction</em>, one can enjoy an ice-cold, delectable root beer float, or grab a juicy <em>1/2-pound burger, grilled and served on a bun with the works.</em> It's a nice place to stop. (Particularly, since it's the only <em>real</em> place between Livermore and San Jose. At least, traveling along <a href="http://maps.google.com/maps/ms?msid=206076442675540335742.0004be503d2e6c56705e9&msa=0" target="_blank">Mines Road</a>.)</p>

One Google Maps reviewer pointedly <a href="http://maps.google.com/maps/place?hl=en&um=1&ie=UTF-8&q=the+junction+mines+road&fb=1&gl=us&hq=the+junction+mines+road&cid=4344605803743098407&ei=etOUT46-A8WsiQKJiPnkDw&sa=X&oi=local_result&ct=placepage-link&resnum=1&ved=0CDUQ4gkwAA" target="_blank">wrote</a>:

<blockquote>
<strong>Why am I telling you about this place?</strong> On a hot day after a long ride through the middle of a most beautiful nowhere... you come upon this ramshackle at the only juncture in the road for almost 50 miles and at this spot lies plenty of cold beer [let's assume he means root beer, to keep this post PG-13]  and grilled burgers.
</blockquote>

<p>I prepared fresh sandwiches <em>to order</em> for the 160 Devil Mountain Double Century bicyclists. And by the end of it, I had it down to a fine art...</p>

<blockquote>
Hi there.
<br/><br/>
We can order you something from inside or I can make you a fresh cold-meat sandwich.
<br/><br/>
White or brown bread?
<br/><br/>
Mustard? Mayonnaise?
<br/><br/>
Tomatoes? Lettuce? Pickles? (Yes, pickles ...sodium rich pickles.)
<br/><br/>
What kind of cheese would you like (Swiss, Monterey Jack, Colby Jack, or Cheedar)?
<br/><br/>
Ham or turkey?
</blockquote>

<p>This was the first Double that I've volunteered at. And I sincerely enjoyed myself. It was nice, for a change, to see the top riders blazing through.</p>

<p>The first rider came in around noon.</p>

<table style="width:auto;"><tr><td><a href="https://plus.google.com/photos/113607174221973452906/albums/5736666246499511073" target="_blank"><img src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/-us0lT5SbK1k/T5y9cleqdxI/AAAAAAAAAOo/U9tOvjNj5Ew/s640/IMG_0712.JPG" height="478" width="640" /></a></td></tr><tr><td style="font-family:arial,sans-serif; font-size:11px; text-align:right">#6: Mr. Rick Ashabranner</td></tr></table>

<p></p>
<p>It's really interesting to contrast the <em>elite</em> riders with the, well, less elite riders. The top guys are <em>in and out</em>. For them, it's obviously a race. Spending the least amount of time at each rest stop gives them a competitive advantage. <em>Yes, seconds matter.</em></p>

<table style="width:auto;" class="image-right"><tr><td><a href="https://plus.google.com/photos/113607174221973452906/albums/5736666246499511073" target="_blank"><img src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/-9TmtP5bW1l0/T5y-izIJ4kI/AAAAAAAAASE/Z7ryKIZbxJY/s400/IMG_0756.JPG" height="299" width="400" /></a></td></tr><tr><td style="font-family:arial,sans-serif; font-size:11px; text-align:right"></td></tr></table>

<p>To help them, we grab their bottles for them and refill them with their choice drink--usually, plain ole' water. The riders meticulously fill their pockets with supplies, they chomp on a banana or some strawberries, and then they're off. <em>Phew</em>.</p>

<p>On the other hand, you have the guys who come <em>rolling</em> in later, with obvious pain, exhaustion, and <em>boy-oh-boy</em>, a ravenous appetite.</p>

<p>For these fine folks, the procedure is simple...</p>

<blockquote>
Me: Hi there. We can order you something from inside or...
<br/><br/>
Bicyclist: <em>Food</em>.
<br/><br/>
Me: Uhm... okay. White or brown...
<br/><br/>
Bicyclist: Yes.
<br/><br/>
Me: Mustard? Mayonnaise?
<br/><br/>
Bicyclist: Sounds great.
<br/><br/>
Me: Ham or turkey?
<br/><br/>
Bicyclist: <em>Wonderful</em>.
</blockquote>

<p>And I'm not trying to make fun of them. <em>I know how they feel</em>. And that's why I put all the love I had into those sandwiches. I made them the way I'd like them: big and packed--<em>no holds barred</em>.</p>

<table style="width:auto;" class="image-left"><tr><td><a href="https://plus.google.com/photos/113607174221973452906/albums/5736666246499511073" target="_blank"><img src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/-OsWl9cV5RZo/T5y9bpqf3GI/AAAAAAAAAOc/YM0ijCS7OpQ/s400/IMG_0709.JPG" height="400" width="299" /></a></td></tr><tr><td style="font-family:arial,sans-serif; font-size:11px; text-align:right"></td></tr></table>

<p>My mom was working behind the scenes, cutting fruit, chopping tomatoes, you name it. She was also very kind in locating ice-cold beverages for the bicyclists from the many ice chests that we had. Coke, Pepsi, Mountain Dew (the super caffeinated type), V8, fruit juice (the extra diabetic type), and <em>root beer</em> (bottled, not canned ...a sure disappointment for many. No, not the bottled or canned thing. The <em>root</em> in front of <em>beer</em>).</p>

<p>My dad dealt the drugs. Namely, Endurolytes (an expensive form of sugar and salt, but concentrated and <em>in a pill form for the down-and-dirty addict</em>). And of course, <a href="http://www.google.com/search?client=safari&rls=en&q=ibuprofen&ie=UTF-8&oe=UTF-8" target="_blank"><em>Vitamin I</em></a>. He also managed the drink powders: Sustained Energy, Heed, and Perpeteum.</p>
